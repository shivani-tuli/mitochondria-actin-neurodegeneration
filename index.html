Mitochondria and the Actin Cytoskeleton in Neurodegeneration

How disruptions in actin-mitochondria crosstalk act as a shared upstream trigger in Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, Huntington's disease, and ALS.

Abstract

Mitochondrial dysfunction and cytoskeletal disorganization are widely recognized hallmarks of neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer's disease (AD), Parkinson's disease (PD), Huntington's disease (HD), and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS). Although these disorders differ in clinical presentation and etiology, accumulating evidence points to a shared cellular vulnerability at the intersection of mitochondrial dynamics and actin cytoskeletal regulation.

In this review, we examine the emerging role of actin-mitochondria crosstalk as a convergent mechanism in neurodegeneration. We discuss how disruptions in actin filament remodeling, mitochondrial fission and fusion, organelle transport, and mitophagy contribute to neuronal dysfunction and loss across these diseases.

Particular attention is given to disease-specific pathways, including cofilin-actin rod formation in AD, α-synuclein-driven actin disruption in PD, mutant huntingtin's effects on mitochondrial fragmentation in HD, and profilin-1-associated mitochondrial defects in ALS.

By synthesizing findings from diverse models, we highlight how perturbations in the cytoskeleton-mitochondria interface may act as an upstream trigger and amplifier of neurodegenerative cascades. We also outline key knowledge gaps and propose future directions for research, with an emphasis on targeting actin-mitochondrial interactions as a potential therapeutic strategy across multiple neurodegenerative conditions.

Key Findings by Disease

🧠

Alzheimer's Disease

Cofilin-actin rod formation blocks axonal transport of mitochondria, creating regions of energy deficit. Amyloid-beta and oxidative stress trigger cofilin dephosphorylation, driving rod assembly and synaptic loss.

⚡

Parkinson's Disease

α-Synuclein aggregation destabilizes actin networks and localizes to mitochondrial membranes, disrupting the electron transport chain and promoting excessive DRP1-mediated fission in dopaminergic neurons.

🔬

Huntington's Disease

Mutant huntingtin (mHTT) increases DRP1 activity while inhibiting fusion proteins (MFN1/2, OPA1), causing mitochondrial fragmentation and disrupting motor protein–cytoskeleton coupling via HAP1-dynein complexes.

🧬

Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis

Profilin-1 (PFN1) mutations disrupt actin polymerization in motor neurons, secondarily impairing mitochondrial axonal transport, reducing membrane potential, and triggering excessive mitophagy.

Convergent Therapeutic Target

Across all four diseases, actin-mitochondria crosstalk disruption represents a shared upstream vulnerability. Strategies targeting this interface — including cofilin inhibitors, fission-fusion modulators, and transport enhancers — could yield disease-modifying therapies applicable across multiple neurodegenerative conditions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is actin-mitochondria crosstalk and why does it matter for neurodegeneration?

Actin-mitochondria crosstalk refers to the bidirectional signaling and physical interactions between the actin cytoskeleton and mitochondria. The actin network regulates mitochondrial fission, fusion, transport, and mitophagy. When this crosstalk is disrupted — as seen in Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, Huntington's disease, and ALS — mitochondria become fragmented, mislocalized, or unable to undergo quality control, leading to energy failure and neuronal death. This review by Tuli et al. (2026) argues that this crosstalk is a shared upstream vulnerability across major neurodegenerative diseases.

How do cofilin-actin rods contribute to Alzheimer's disease?

In Alzheimer's disease, oxidative stress and amyloid-beta (Aβ) exposure trigger the dephosphorylation of cofilin, causing it to bind saturated actin filaments and form cofilin-actin rod structures. These rods block axonal and dendritic transport of mitochondria, creating regions of energy deficit. They also sequester cofilin, disrupting normal actin dynamics needed for synaptic plasticity. This dual disruption — transport blockade plus cytoskeletal collapse — accelerates synaptic loss and cognitive decline.

What role does α-synuclein play in actin and mitochondrial disruption in Parkinson's disease?

Alpha-synuclein (α-syn), the protein that aggregates into Lewy bodies in Parkinson's disease, directly interacts with both actin filaments and mitochondrial membranes. Mutant or aggregated α-syn destabilizes actin networks, impairing mitochondrial motility along cytoskeletal tracks. It also localizes to mitochondrial membranes, disrupting electron transport chain complexes and promoting excessive mitochondrial fission via DRP1 activation. Together, these effects create a vicious cycle of cytoskeletal disorganization and mitochondrial dysfunction in dopaminergic neurons.

How does mutant huntingtin cause mitochondrial fragmentation in Huntington's disease?

Mutant huntingtin (mHTT) with expanded CAG repeats promotes excessive mitochondrial fission by increasing DRP1 activity while inhibiting fusion proteins (MFN1/2 and OPA1). mHTT also disrupts actin-dependent mitochondrial transport by interfering with motor protein–cytoskeleton coupling, particularly through HAP1-dynein and kinesin complexes. The resulting mitochondrial fragmentation and mis-localization deprives neurons of adequate ATP at synaptic terminals, contributing to the progressive motor and cognitive symptoms of Huntington's disease.

What is the connection between profilin-1 and mitochondrial defects in ALS?

Profilin-1 (PFN1) is an actin-binding protein that regulates actin polymerization. Mutations in PFN1 are linked to familial ALS. Mutant PFN1 disrupts normal actin dynamics in motor neurons, which secondarily impairs mitochondrial transport along axons, reduces mitochondrial membrane potential, and triggers excessive mitophagy. Since motor neurons have exceptionally long axons, they are particularly vulnerable to these transport defects. The review by Tuli et al. highlights PFN1 as a critical node linking cytoskeletal integrity to mitochondrial health in ALS.

Could targeting actin-mitochondrial interactions be a therapeutic strategy for neurodegenerative diseases?

Yes. Because actin-mitochondria crosstalk is disrupted across multiple neurodegenerative diseases (AD, PD, HD, ALS), it represents a promising convergent therapeutic target. Strategies under investigation include small molecules that stabilize actin dynamics (e.g., cofilin inhibitors), compounds that restore mitochondrial fission-fusion balance, and agents that enhance mitochondrial transport. The review emphasizes that targeting this shared cellular interface could yield disease-modifying therapies applicable across multiple neurodegenerative conditions, rather than treating each disease in isolation.
